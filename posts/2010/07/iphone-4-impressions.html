iPhone 4 Impressions

July 31 2010, 10:10 PM  by AJ Bourg

As you all know, I had an HTC EVO 4G for a month which I returned under Sprint’s great 30 day money back guarantee. And much to Sprint’s credit, they gave me no real hassle and returned my money. My experience with Sprint, despite the lackluster phone, couldn’t have been better.

I replaced that phone with the iPhone 4 and have been giving it its workout for the same 30 days and wanted to tell you what I like and don’t like.

So let’s just hop in.

The Good

  • Love the speed improvements. They’ve really optimized the right actions and made it feel much much faster than before. Especially when switching apps and using the camera app.

  • Love the new multiple Exchange account feature. Before, I could only have one push Gmail account because push Gmail uses the Exchange Active Sync protocol. Now I can have my personal email pushed as well as my work email.

  • I really like the new design. The previous plastic design feels so cheap and shoddy in comparison. I wasn’t a fan of the new design at first, but the steel and glass has really grown on me.

  • Folders! About time. My home screen was just a mess without folders.

  • Nice, high quality camera. Love how the new camera performs. I stopped carrying my point and shoot with me thanks to the iPhone camera.

  • Battery life. I easily get a day out of it, sometimes a day and a half depending on use. The EVO could not compare at all.

  • The headphone remote. I really like being able to place calls, end calls, listen to music, change tracks, etc with just the headphones. Can keep the phone in my pocket while I am talking on the phone.

  • The AT&T A-List. I signed up for the more expensive plan and got free 5 phone numbers I could call any time. Given that there’s probably no more than 3-4 numbers I call regularly, this is a great deal. Then I have 900 minutes with rollover I can use for all other situations.

  • Awesome awesome apps. Despite Apple’s developer-unfriendly system, there are a ton of great apps and they are all miles ahead of the competition. Some of my favorite apps right now: Gowalla, Twitter, Instapaper, Things, Evernote and, of course, the YouVersion App. (full disclosure: I work there) Even when there are Android equivalents, I think the iPhone app is better. For example, I think Evernote on Android is an UI disaster, but awesome on iPhone.

The Not So Good

  • The whole antenna issue. It’s something of an issue, if I hold my iPhone just right I can see the bars go down significantly. However, for my use, the antenna issue hasn’t impaired my phone use one bit. I haven’t had a single dropped call while holding the phone so I am not too worried about it. (more on dropped calls in a bit)

  • I am really worried about its fragility. I am concerned that I am going to drop it and shatter it in a million pieces, Worse, at home repair is going to be made more difficult by the fact that the screen is glued to the glass. I’ve replaced 3G screens myself with no trouble, but this worries me just a little bit.

The Ugly

  • AT&T. You knew this was coming. Their network sucks, their prices are too high, and you have to pay an extra $20 a month to get unlimited text messaging. A service that costs them nothing.

  • The buying experience. Surprisingly, it took me an hour and a half at the Apple store to buy an iPhone. I’ve never had a bad experience at an Apple store and so this was my first time having any issues at all at Apple. It’s not really Apple’s fault though because AT&T rejected my order. I don’t know why, probably because I had a Colorado billing address but was buying/activating it in Oklahoma City. Best part of the day was when the Apple store guy called the AT&T guy a “douche” and then took me back to the super secret employee-only section.

    In contrast, I was in and out of the Sprint store in like 15 minutes. (no one was there…) I told them I wanted a Colorado number and they didn’t bat an eye. And Sprint gave me the correct area code (303) for my billing address, while AT&T gave me a long distance area code (970). Sigh.

  • Dropped calls. I think this is more in line with my location at the time, but I had a terrible time with dropped calls for a while in one particular (2nd floor) room. It wasn’t the Antennagate issue because I never held the phone, and always was using headphones while doing something else. Fortunately, I have not yet had this issue since I’ve been back in Colorado. I think it was just one dead spot I was in in OKlahoma that caused the issue. (but I never had a single issue with Sprint dropping any calls anywhere)

What iPhone Could Learn From Android

All that said, I think the iPhone is the best phone available. If you can put up with AT&T (I can) then I don’t think it’s even a question: get the iPhone.

But, despite my criticism, Android is a great system and for many people, they will love it. There are a number of things that Android does really well that I think the iPhone can learn from.

  • Better cooperation. This is something everybody learns in pre-school. Each app on iPhone feels like a discreet unit without any context or association with any other app. This needs to change. Apps need to be able to interact with each other better. A perfect example of this is in the camera app. On Android, I can take a picture and hit a sharing button and it will ask me which app to share the picture with. It will show me any app I’ve installed that can handle pictures, like all of my Twitter clients.

    iPhone, not so much. I can email the photo, but that’s about it. To share it, I have to open whatever program I want to use, and then find the picture I wanted to share. Lots of context switching, not much sharing.

    I think iPhone is slowly working its way there, the new OS will ask you which application to open a Word doc emailed to you, for example. I am sure Apple will eventually expand this api to other services, but until then, I have to keep reminding myself where to go.

  • Better app shortcut management. I hate having every single icon on my home screen. Most of my apps sit unused. Folders helps this somewhat, but in someways, it makes it more of a pain. In Android, I only had two home screens of icons I used frequently. Everything else I just accessed through the All Apps button. I would love that on my iPhone.

  • Widgets. I am still shocked that Apple hasn’t created widgets for iOS. It’s such a no-brainer. I really liked being able to have a selection of weather, clock, calendar, Twitter, Facebook, etc widgets I could put straight on the home screen. Apple already has the technology on the Mac.

    This is so obvious to me, that I think one of the next few releases (potentially for WWDC ‘11) will be widget support on the iPhone, a widget store in iTunes, and native support in Dashcode. It’s an obvious line extension, and could be a nice couple hundred million add on for iTunes.

    But I am a terrible predictor of Jobs.

  • A loosened collar. Sorry Apple, you need to stop being so anti-competitive and loosen your grip a bit on the whole ecosystem. I don’t think they’re in any danger of losing their lead anytime soon (Jobs has been through this before, and is older and wiser and I think is a couple steps ahead of the rest of us) but they are going to face increasing competitive and legislative pressure for their measures soon.

Conclusion

I had an iPhone 3G for a while and really liked it. iPhone 4 is a big upgrade, and solved all of the complaints I had with a 3G. I have been very happy with my iPhone 4, and you can pry it from my cold, dead hands.
